Essay Tips (Complete Guide)

Clear, honest writing is the strongest strategy. This guide explains what to write, why it works, and how to do it step by step.

1) What an essay really is

An essay is a short story about your thinking. Readers want to see how you solve problems, not how many big words you know.

Admissions readers look for: clarity, honesty, and proof of growth.

Why honesty matters: “I failed my first robotics test, then redesigned the sensor and improved accuracy.” This shows learning, not perfection.

2) Types of essays

Personal statement — Purpose: your identity and growth. Used for universities. Example: “How building a water filter changed my view of engineering.”

Motivation essay — Purpose: why you want the opportunity. Used for scholarships/programs. Example: “Why I want this AI summer program.”

Why this program — Purpose: fit and goals. Example: “How your lab aligns with my bioelectronics project.”

Academic interest — Purpose: show subject passion. Example: “My curiosity about climate data.”

Leadership / impact — Purpose: influence on others. Example: “Leading a STEM club to tutor younger students.”

Short-answer — Purpose: quick clarity. Example: “Describe one challenge you solved.”

3) Essay structure

Introduction: One focused idea. Mistake: vague or very general. Better: a specific moment.

Example: “At 2 a.m., our sensor failed, and I had 12 hours to fix it.”

Body: Explain actions and results. Mistake: listing achievements. Better: show decisions and evidence.

Example: “I tested three materials, recorded the error rate, and chose the best one.”

Conclusion: Reflect and connect to your goal. Mistake: repeat the intro. Better: show next step.

Example: “This project made me want to study biomedical engineering to build affordable tools.”

4) How to start strong

Good openings: short story, specific moment, observation, or question.

Good: “When the power went out during our experiment, I learned how fragile data can be.”

Bad openings: dictionary definitions, very general sentences, “Since I was young…”.

Bad: “Science is very important in the world.” (Too general, no story.)

5) Body paragraphs (depth)

One idea per paragraph. Explain it with evidence.

Show, don’t tell. Use actions and results.

Bad: “I am a leader.”
Good: “I organized weekly sessions, assigned roles, and tracked progress.”

Link paragraphs smoothly: Use short connectors like “Because of this…” or “This led me to…”.

6) Conclusions

Do not repeat. Add a final insight and connect to your future.

Example: “Now I want to study data science to predict floods, not just analyze them.”

7) Language & style

  • Clear sentences beat fancy words.
  • Use active voice: “I built” not “was built.”
  • Vary sentence length to keep flow.
  • Formal but human tone — be direct.
  • Avoid repetition by using precise verbs.

8) Common essay mistakes

Listing achievements — Example: “I won X, Y, Z.” Fix: choose one story and explain impact.

Rewriting the CV — Example: long lists of activities. Fix: show one decision and result.

Big vocabulary — Example: “I endeavored to…” Fix: “I tried to…”

Weak grammar — Example: “I do experiment.” Fix: “I ran an experiment.”

No clear message — Example: many topics. Fix: one main idea in every paragraph.

9) Step-by-step process

  1. Brainstorming: list 5 moments where you learned something real.
  2. Choose ONE idea: pick the story with the strongest result.
  3. Outline: intro → 2–3 body paragraphs → conclusion.
  4. First draft: write freely; fix later.
  5. Editing: cut weak sentences and add clear evidence.
  6. Final review: read aloud for clarity.

10) Final checklist

  • Is the main idea clear in the first paragraph?
  • Does each paragraph support that idea?
  • Is the language clear and correct?
  • Does it sound like me, not a template?
  • Can I show one strong example?
